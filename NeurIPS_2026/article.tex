\begin{abstract}
  World models in multi-agent environments must simultaneously capture structured transition dynamics and residual uncertainty arising from partial observability, stochasticity, and agent interactions. We show that learning these components jointly with a single predictive model leads to a fundamental failure mode: rollouts can remain numerically accurate while violating semantic transition constraints, producing unrealistic trajectories that degrade planning performance over long horizons.

  We propose a decompositional view of multi-agent world modeling in which transition prediction is explicitly factorized into \emph{rule-covered} components, governed by known structural invariants, and \emph{residual} components that must be learned from data. This perspective yields Neuro-Symbolic Multi-Agent World Models (NS-MAWM), a backbone-agnostic framework that exposes this decomposition at the representation level and induces a unified design space spanning soft regularization, hard projection, and residual symbolic dynamics.

  To evaluate semantic reliability beyond standard prediction metrics, we introduce Rule Violation Rate (RVR), which distinguishes learned coherence from enforced validity and quantifies the extent of structural inconsistencies in model rollouts. Across Gridcraft, Overcooked, Predator--Prey, and SMACv2, NS-MAWM reduces long-horizon prediction error by up to [33.6\%], decreases learned rule violations from [0.194 $\pm$ 0.021] to [0.071 $\pm$ 0.012] under soft integration, eliminates violations on covered features under hard modes, and improves matched-budget MPC/CEM planning return from [41.2 $\pm$ 3.8] to [57.6 $\pm$ 2.7] while substantially reducing invalid imagined plans.

  These results support the view that separating structured and residual dynamics is key to building semantically reliable world models for multi-agent decision-making. Rather than treating symbolic rules as a post-hoc repair, NS-MAWM treats them as a decomposition of the learning target into covered and residual transition components. As a secondary exploratory study, we also examine whether NS-MAWM can use imperfect symbolic rules generated by an LLM, providing a first step toward partially automated symbolic coverage.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Learning predictive models of environment dynamics, known as \textbf{world models} (WMs)~\cite{ha2018worldmodels}, is a central paradigm in \textbf{model-based reinforcement learning} (MBRL)~\cite{hafner2019learning,hafner2020dreamer,hafner2021dreamerv2}. By enabling imagination-based rollouts, WMs support planning, policy learning, and long-horizon reasoning~\cite{schrittwieser2020}.

In \textbf{multi-agent reinforcement learning} (MARL), however, world modeling faces a fundamental challenge: transition dynamics arise from the interaction of two qualitatively different components. On one hand, environments exhibit \emph{structured regularities} that follow deterministic or rule-governed patterns (e.g., spatial constraints, exclusivity conditions, resource conservation). On the other hand, they contain \emph{residual uncertainty} induced by partial observability, stochasticity, hidden variables, and the behavior of other agents~\cite{Wong2023,Venugopal2024MABL,agravante-etal-2023-learning}.

\paragraph{A failure mode of joint modeling.}
Standard world models attempt to learn these components jointly with a single predictive function. We argue that this leads to a fundamental failure mode: a model can achieve low reconstruction or likelihood error while producing semantically invalid transitions. For example, rollouts may place multiple agents in exclusive locations, duplicate or remove objects without interaction, or allow inactive agents to act. These inconsistencies are often invisible to standard prediction metrics, yet they accumulate during open-loop rollouts and can severely degrade downstream planning~\cite{talvitie2014modelbias,venkatraman2015improving}.

This observation highlights a mismatch between \emph{statistical accuracy} and \emph{semantic validity}. A world model can be accurate in expectation while generating trajectories that violate the structural rules of the environment. In multi-agent settings, where rollouts are iteratively fed back into the model, such violations compound and lead to unreliable decision-making.

\paragraph{A decompositional perspective.}
We propose that this failure stems from attempting to learn structured and unstructured dynamics within a single representation. Instead, we advocate a \emph{decompositional view} of world modeling, in which transition prediction is factorized into:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{rule-covered components}, corresponding to features whose evolution is governed by known symbolic transition rules, and
  \item \textbf{residual components}, capturing uncertainty that must be learned from data.
\end{itemize}

Formally, this perspective separates predictable transition structure from learned uncertainty, rather than forcing a single model to approximate both. The key question is whether such a decomposition can improve the \emph{semantic reliability} of multi-agent world models: their ability to generate long-horizon rollouts that remain both accurate and structurally valid under partial observability.

\paragraph{Neuro-symbolic world modeling under partial predictability.}
Many multi-agent domains provide partial transition knowledge prior to learning. For instance, agents cannot occupy the same exclusive location, unavailable actions should not alter the state, and bounded resources should not change arbitrarily. These constraints define \emph{admissible transitions}, independently of reward or optimality. Learning them purely from data is inefficient and brittle, especially under limited coverage and long rollout horizons.

This motivates a neuro-symbolic approach in which symbolic transition rules are integrated directly into the world model. Crucially, we do not treat symbolic knowledge as a post-hoc correction or external constraint, but as a \emph{structural component of the transition representation itself}. This leads to a representation-level separation between covered and residual dynamics.

\paragraph{Robustness to imperfect symbolic knowledge.}
While NS-MAWM assumes that rules are correct, in practice symbolic knowledge is often incomplete or noisy. In this paper, the LLM experiment is presented explicitly as a
\emph{robustness test} rather than as a rule-generation contribution. We conduct an exploratory evaluation where symbolic rules are generated automatically by an LLM and then filtered minimally for consistency. The decomposition framework naturally supports this: if rules are imperfect, the residual neural component can compensate through learned error correction.
This is not a contribution to LLM-based rule generation, but rather a validation that the decomposition interface degrades gracefully under noisy inputs.
We test two conditions: (1) LLM-Raw: rules parsed directly from LLM output; (2) LLM-Refined: rules after lightweight filtering for consistency. By measuring RVR, correction magnitude, and planning return under both conditions, we show that NS-MAWM remains useful even when symbolic coverage is partial and approximate---provided the neural residual component is informative and the integration mode (soft vs. hard) is chosen accordingly.

\paragraph{Contribution.}
The core insight of this work is that partial predictability in multi-agent dynamics should be formalized as a
\emph{decomposition problem}, not as a system of symbolic heuristics layered on top of a learned model.
We introduce \textbf{Neuro-Symbolic Multi-Agent World Models (NS-MAWM)}, a backbone-agnostic framework that operationalizes this decomposition. By explicitly separating what can be predicted by symbolic rules from what must be learned from data, NS-MAWM reframes world modeling from ``learn everything jointly'' to ``remove what is structurally predictable from the neural learning target.''
This yields three concrete contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a \textbf{conceptual contribution}: a formal decomposition of multi-agent transition dynamics into rule-covered (structurally predictable) and residual (empirically learned) components, making partial symbolic coverage the central inductive bias;
  \item a \textbf{methodological contribution}: a unified design space spanning soft regularization, hard projection, and residual factorization, showing how to couple symbolic and neural components at different levels of coupling strength;
  \item a \textbf{diagnostic contribution}: an evaluation framework (Rule Violation Rate, correction magnitude, downstream planning) that distinguishes learned coherence from enforced validity, preventing misinterpretation of zero violations achieved through late-stage correction.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why decomposition matters beyond projection.}
One might ask: if hard projection enforces validity at inference, why decompose the learning target itself? The answer reveals the core advantage of our approach. Naive projection fixes invalid outputs \emph{after} the neural model generates them---it does not change what the model is asked to learn. Residual symbolic dynamics, by contrast, removes covered features from the neural learning target entirely, reducing the hypothesis space for residual components. This achieves three distinct benefits: (i) faster learning in data-limited regimes, because the neural model no longer wastes capacity on deterministic structure; (ii) better generalization, because the model learns only the truly variable, environment-specific aspects; and (iii) cleaner diagnostics, because small RVR$_{\text{pre}}$ and small $\Delta_{\text{corr}}$ together indicate that the decomposition is working, not that we are patching arbitrary neural failures. This distinction between output correction and learning-target reframing is what makes decomposition more powerful than projection alone.

\paragraph{From prediction accuracy to semantic reliability.}
We define \emph{semantic reliability} as the ability of a world model to generate rollouts that are simultaneously accurate, structurally valid, and useful for downstream decision-making. To evaluate this property, we go beyond standard prediction metrics and analyze:
(i) long-horizon error accumulation,
(ii) rule violations before and after enforcement,
(iii) correction magnitude under hard constraints, and
(iv) downstream planning performance under matched computational budgets.

\paragraph{Empirical validation.}
We evaluate NS-MAWM on Gridcraft, Overcooked~\cite{Micah2020overcooked}, Predator--Prey~\cite{lowe2017multi}, and SMACv2~\cite{Ellis2023smacv2}. Results show that decomposing transition dynamics improves long-horizon prediction, reduces semantic violations, and leads to more reliable model-based planning. In particular, symbolic structure reduces violation rates and slows error accumulation, while preserving or improving downstream performance even under partial symbolic coverage.

\paragraph{Systematic evaluation axes.}
To test the decomposition hypothesis, we structure our evaluation along three axes.
\emph{Symbolic coverage} ranges from $\kappa=0$ (purely neural baseline) through partial manual coverage ($\kappa \approx 0.18$--$0.42$ depending on environment) to an oracle baseline with complete symbolic knowledge.
\emph{Integration regime} spans three mechanisms for coupling symbolic and neural components: soft regularization during training, hard projection at inference time, and factorization where covered features are predicted symbolically and residual features neurally.
\emph{World model backbone} holds fixed to a shared MAWM architecture and validates that improvements transfer across standard structured and latent-variable alternatives.
Together, these axes structure the evaluation as a hypothesis-testing study rather than a collection of available baselines, demonstrating that semantic reliability emerges consistently when rule-covered structure is separated from residual uncertainty.

\paragraph{Main claim.}
Our results support a central claim: \emph{world models that explicitly separate structured and residual dynamics are more semantically reliable than models that learn both jointly}. This suggests that decomposition, rather than increasing model capacity alone, is key to robust multi-agent world modeling.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
We analyze prior work through the sub-objectives introduced in \autoref{sec:intro}. The goal of this section is not only to position NS-MAWM, but to show why the three gaps are necessary obstacles to semantically reliable multi-agent WMs.

\paragraph{World models and structured dynamics.}
PlaNet and Dreamer-style WMs~\cite{hafner2019learning,hafner2020dreamer,hafner2021dreamerv2} learn powerful latent dynamics, but their standard formulations are not designed around multi-agent entity structure or symbolic constraints. NS-MAWM is orthogonal to this line of work: an RSSM/Dreamer-style latent transition model can serve as the neural backbone when the symbolic predicates needed by the coverage mask are decoded or supervised. Structured WMs, including graph-based WMs~\cite{zhang2021worldmodelgraph} and contrastive structured WMs~\cite{kipf2020contrastivelearningstructuredworld}, introduce stronger representational priors, but typically do not evaluate symbolic rule satisfaction as a central metric.
This line motivates \textbf{G1}: structure is useful, but existing WM formalisms do not explicitly separate symbolically covered and residual multi-agent observation features.

\paragraph{Model-based MARL.}
Model-based MARL methods address partial observability, joint action effects, and sample efficiency~\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning,Moerland2023modelbased}. MABL~\cite{Venugopal2024MABL} and MAMBPO~\cite{Willemsen2021MAMBPO} introduce multi-agent structure into model learning and policy optimization, while other approaches model opponent or interaction dynamics~\cite{Xu2022mingling,Yuxiaopeng2022modelbased,nekoei2023dealing}. These works motivate evaluating downstream control, but they generally do not treat symbolic coherence of imagined rollouts as an explicit optimization and diagnostic target.
This motivates \textbf{G3}: downstream decision quality is central in model-based MARL, but the causal link between semantic rollout validity and planning/control quality remains under-evaluated.

\paragraph{Neuro-symbolic and constrained learning.}
Semantic loss~\cite{Xu2018semanticloss}, differentiable logic rules~\cite{Fan2017differentiablelearninglogicalrules}, and probabilistic logic systems such as DeepProbLog~\cite{manhaeve2018deepproblog} show how symbolic knowledge can regularize neural learning. Related constrained-learning paradigms, including physics-informed neural networks~\cite{raissi2019physicsinformed}, also integrate prior knowledge through loss terms, architecture choices, or output projection. NS-MAWM follows the same broad principle but targets a different failure mode: semantic drift in multi-agent rollout generation under partial observability and partial symbolic coverage. Neuro-symbolic WMs such as WorldCloner~\cite{balloch2023neurosymbolicworldmodelsadapting} and related approaches~\cite{agravante-etal-2023-learning} bring symbolic structure closer to transition modeling. However, the multi-agent setting raises additional questions about agent-indexed observations, rule coverage masks, and whether hard symbolic consistency improves downstream planning rather than only prediction.
This motivates \textbf{G2}: existing neuro-symbolic mechanisms do not provide a unified multi-agent WM interface for soft regularization, hard projection, and residual symbolic dynamics under partial symbolic coverage.

\paragraph{Evaluation beyond prediction loss.}
Most WM evaluations emphasize reconstruction, prediction error, sample efficiency, or downstream reward. Related evaluation efforts argue for broader diagnostics of generative and world models~\cite{Duan_2025_ICCV,Delvecchio2025p1157}. For multi-agent WMs, we argue that such diagnostics should explicitly include pre-correction symbolic violations, post-correction guarantees, correction magnitude, rule coverage, robustness to incomplete symbolic knowledge, and OOD structural generalization.
This reinforces \textbf{G3}: without diagnostics that distinguish learned coherence from enforced coherence, a zero violation rate can be mistaken for a globally accurate or useful world model.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

We introduce the notation needed for the method before defining NS-MAWM, with particular attention to joint observations, joint actions, histories, learned dynamics, and the distinction between prediction, rollout generation, and downstream control.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning}
\label{sec:decpomdp}

To study cooperative \textbf{Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning} (MARL) under partial observability, we adopt the \textbf{Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process} (Dec-POMDP) formalism~\cite{Oliehoek2016}, which provides a principled framework for decentralized decision-making where agents act on local observations while jointly optimizing a shared objective.
Dec-POMDPs are a strict subclass of \textbf{Partially Observable Stochastic Games}, specialized to fully cooperative settings with a common reward and a jointly optimized policy~\cite{Beynier2013}.

Formally, a Dec-POMDP is a 7-tuple
$d = \langle S, \{A_i\}_{i=1}^n, T, R, \{\Omega_i\}_{i=1}^n, O, \gamma \rangle$,
where $S$ is the state space;
$A_i$ and $\Omega_i$ are the action and observation spaces of agent $i$;
$A^j=A_1\times\dots\times A_n$ is the joint action space;
$\Omega^j=\Omega_1\times\dots\times\Omega_n$ is the joint observation space;
$T(s,a,s') = \mathbb{P}(s' \mid s,a)$ is the transition function for joint actions
$a = (a_1,\dots,a_n)\in A^j$;
$R : S \times A^j \times S \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is the shared reward;
$O(s',a,\omega) = \mathbb{P}(\omega \mid s',a)$ is the observation function;
and $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor.

Each agent $i \in \mathcal{A} = \{1,\dots,n\}$ follows a (possibly stochastic) policy
$\pi_i : \Omega_i \rightarrow \Delta(A_i)$,
where $\Delta(A_i)$ denotes the probability simplex over actions in $A_i$,
and the \textbf{joint policy} is
$\pi^j = (\pi_1,\dots,\pi_n)$.
Let $H_i$ denote agent histories
$h_t^i = \langle (\omega_\tau^i, a_\tau^i) \rangle_{\tau=0}^{t}$,
and $H^j = H_1 \times \dots \times H_n$ the set of joint histories.
The objective is to find $\pi^j$ maximizing the expected return over a finite horizon $T$; for the infinite-horizon discounted case, assume $\gamma < 1$ and take $T\rightarrow\infty$:
\begin{equation}
  V(\pi^j) =
  \mathbb{E}_{\pi^j}
  \left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}) \right],
  \label{eq:expected_return}
\end{equation}
where the factor $\gamma^t$ follows the standard discounted-return convention and gives lower weight to rewards farther in the future.

\subsection{Model-Based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning}
\label{sec:mb-marl}

\textbf{Model-Based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning} (MB-MARL) extends MBRL to multi-agent decision-making.
The central idea is to learn an approximate environment model
and exploit it for planning or policy optimization,
thereby improving sample efficiency and coordination
\cite{Moerland2023modelbased,Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning}.

\paragraph{Environment models.}
In cooperative settings with $n$ agents, dynamics are governed by
$P(s' \mid s, a_1,\dots,a_n)$ with shared reward $R(s,a_1,\dots,a_n)$.
MB-MARL learns approximations
$\hat{P}_\phi$ and $\hat{R}_\phi$ from interaction data.
$\hat{P}_\phi$ is a parameterized transition predictor, and
$\hat{R}_\phi$ is a parameterized reward predictor used for planning or
policy improvement when rewards are not directly observed in imagined rollouts.
Compared to single-agent MBRL, learning such models is more challenging
due to joint action spaces, partial observability, and learning-induced non-stationarity
\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning}.

\paragraph{Model usage.}
Following standard taxonomies \cite{Moerland2023modelbased,Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning},
learned models are used for
(i) planning (such as \textbf{Model Predictive Control} -- MPC),
(ii) Dyna-style learning with short imagined rollouts,
or (iii) direct policy optimization in the learned model,
which is sample-efficient but sensitive to model bias
\cite{Moerland2023modelbased}.

\paragraph{Model structure.}
MB-MARL methods differ by model structure: centralized models learn joint dynamics using global information, similar to \textbf{Centralized Training and Decentralized Execution} (CTDE) but limited in scalability, whereas decentralized or factorized models learn local or agent-specific dynamics, possibly leveraging communication or structural assumptions~\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning}.

Under assumptions such as bounded rewards, sufficient learned-model accuracy, and planning procedures that are robust to compounding model error, reusing data through model rollouts
can reduce environment interactions and improve sample complexity
in cooperative or discounted stochastic games
\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning,Moerland2023modelbased}.




\subsection{World Models}
\label{sec:wm_basics}

WMs are a class of MBRL methods
that learn environment dynamics~\cite{ha2018worldmodels,hafner2020dreamer}.
They are particularly suited to partially observable settings such as Dec-POMDPs,
enabling planning, improved sample efficiency, and imagination-based reasoning.
Here, we present a simplified deterministic formulation while noting that
modern WMs typically rely on stochastic latent variables trained via variational inference~\cite{hafner2019learning,hafner2020dreamer}.

Formally, at each time step $t$, let $\omega_t \in \Omega$ denote the observation,
$a_t \in A$ the executed action, and $\tilde{h}_{t-1} \in \mathcal{S}_h$ the recurrent hidden state summarizing the interaction history up to time $t-1$. An encoder $Enc : \Omega \rightarrow Z$ maps high-dimensional observations to a compact latent representation $z_t = Enc(\omega_t)$.
%
The temporal evolution of the latent state is modeled by:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{h}_t = f_{\theta}(\tilde{h}_{t-1}, z_t, a_t), \qquad \hat{z}_{t+1} = g_{\theta}(\tilde{h}_t),
  \label{eq:wm_latent_update}
\end{equation}
where $f_{\theta}(\cdot)$ is typically implemented as a Recurrent Neural Network,
such as an \textbf{Long-Short-Term Memory} (LSTM)~\cite{hochreiter1997long},
and $g_{\theta}(\cdot)$ is a parametric function such as \textbf{Multi-Layer Perceptron} (MLP).
%
The predicted latent state is decoded by
$Dec : Z \rightarrow \Omega$ to produce the predicted observation
$\hat{\omega}_{t+1} = Dec(\hat{z}_{t+1})$.

\subsection{Multi-Agent World Models}
\label{sec:mawm_basics}

A common extension of WMs to multi-agent settings consists in learning a centralized latent dynamics model over joint observations and joint actions, similar to CTDE paradigms~\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning}.

Given a dataset of joint trajectories $\mathcal{D}_{H^j} = \{ h^j \}$, with $h^j = \langle (\omega_t^j, a_t^j) \rangle_{t=0}^{T}$, the objective is to learn a predictive model of joint observation dynamics, as commonly done in MB-MARL~\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning,Xu2022mingling}.
%
We define a \textbf{Joint-Observation Prediction Model} (JOPM) as a neural model to capture the environment dynamics:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}_{\theta}^j :
  H^j \times \Omega^j \times A^j
  \rightarrow \Omega^j .
  \label{eq:jopm_type}
\end{equation}
%
The recurrent hidden state $\tilde{h}_t^j\in\mathcal{S}_h$ is distinct from the joint interaction history $h_t^j\in H^j$: the former is the neural state maintained by the JOPM, whereas the latter denotes the sequence of observed joint interactions. With $\operatorname{vec}(\cdot)$ denoting vectorization of structured joint observations or actions, a compact implementation is:
\begin{align}
  z_t^j                & = Enc^j(\operatorname{vec}(\omega_t^j)), \label{eq:mawm_encode}                              \\
  \tilde{h}_t^j        & = f_{\theta}(\tilde{h}_{t-1}^j, z_t^j, \operatorname{vec}(a_t^j)), \label{eq:mawm_recurrent} \\
  \hat{\omega}_{t+1}^j & = Dec^j(g_{\theta}(\tilde{h}_t^j)). \label{eq:mawm_decode}
\end{align}
This three-step form is equivalent to the usual encoder--recurrent-core--decoder architecture while avoiding implementation-level nesting of flattening and unflattening operators in the main formalism.
%
The encoder--decoder pair is typically implemented using MLP or attention-based architectures
to aggregate multi-agent information and capture inter-agent dependencies
\cite{Xu2022mingling,Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning}.
This centralized formulation enables accurate joint dynamics modeling under centralized training,
but suffers from scalability and decentralized execution limitations
\cite{Wang2022modelbasedmultiagentreinforcementlearning,Moerland2023modelbased}.


\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

We now state the problem targeted by NS-MAWM. The goal is not to learn a complete symbolic simulator, nor to replace neural dynamics. Rather, we assume that multi-agent observations contain both rule-covered components and residual components, and we ask how a learned WM can use partial symbolic transition knowledge to generate semantically reliable rollouts.

\paragraph{Inputs and outputs.}
Given a dataset of joint trajectories
$\mathcal{D}=\{(\omega^j_t,a^j_t,\omega^j_{t+1})\}$,
a neural MAWM learns a transition model
$\mathcal{T}^j_\theta(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)$
that predicts $\hat{\omega}^j_{t+1}$.
NS-MAWM additionally receives a symbolic rule set $\mathcal{R}$ defining a partial transition operator $\mathcal{T}^j_s$ on a subset of observation features. Its output is a final prediction $\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1}$ that combines neural and symbolic predictions according to one of the integration modes in \autoref{sec:strategies}.

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
We make four explicit assumptions. First, observations are available as structured feature vectors, or can be encoded into such vectors. Second, only a subset of features is covered by symbolic transition rules; no complete symbolic simulator is assumed. Third, rules may be incomplete, so uncovered features must remain neurally modeled. Fourth, formal guarantees are conditional on rule correctness and mask correctness; when rules are wrong or incomplete, their effect must be evaluated empirically.

\paragraph{Semantic reliability objective.}
Let $\hat{\tau}_{1:H}$ be an open-loop rollout generated by a WM over horizon $H$. A semantically reliable rollout should satisfy three requirements: it should remain close to observed trajectories under predictive metrics, it should satisfy symbolic constraints on covered features, and it should remain useful for downstream model-based decision making. NS-MAWM therefore targets a joint objective: minimize predictive error on all features, minimize rule violations on covered features, and preserve rollout utility for planning/control. The first and third requirements are empirical; the covered-feature validity of hard symbolic integration admits the conditional guarantee in \autoref{sec:formal_properties}.


\section{Neuro-Symbolic Multi-Agent World Models}
\label{sec:method}

We propose \textbf{NS-MAWM}, a framework that implements the covered/residual view of multi-agent world modeling. The method treats partial symbolic knowledge as a statement about transition predictability: some next-observation components are specified by rules, while the remaining components are residual targets for the neural WM. All mechanisms below---regularization, projection, and residual dynamics---are different ways of using this same decomposition, not separate ad hoc modules. A schematic overview of NS-MAWM is provided in \autoref{fig:ns-mawm_overview}, and \autoref{alg:ns_mawm} summarizes the training and rollout procedure.

\subsection{Structured Observation Latent Space and Unified Neuro-Symbolic Formalism}
\label{sec:ns}

To integrate symbolic reasoning into \textbf{Multi-Agent WMs} (MAWMs), we propose a formal decomposition of individual observations and define a symbolic transition operator capable of partially predicting future observations.

\paragraph{Observation representation.} We assume that the observation of an agent $i$ at time $t$ can be expressed as a structured feature vector:
\begin{equation}
  \omega^i_t = (f^i_{t,1}, f^i_{t,2}, \dots, f^i_{t,n_f}),
  \label{eq:structured_observation}
\end{equation}
where each $f^i_{t,k}$ (with $1 \leq k \leq n_f$) is a numerical or categorical feature conveying a specific perceptual attribute, such as relative position to another agent, object type in view, health, resource state, or action-availability/cooldown status. Cooldown-like constraints refer to features that can change only after a valid action and then remain unavailable for a fixed or state-dependent number of steps.

\paragraph{Symbolically predictable features.} We posit that certain features are \emph{symbolically predictable}: their next values can be inferred from the joint history $h^j_{t-1}$, joint observation $\omega^j_t$, joint action $a^j_t$, and a set of symbolic transition rules. These features are governed by invariants or regularities, such as spatial consistency, object persistence, bounded motion, conservation-like inventory constraints, cooldown logic, or action-conditioned transitions. For example, in a gridworld, if an agent moves south and no obstacle blocks the action, then its relative position in nearby observations changes according to a deterministic spatial rule; if the action is blocked, the corresponding position feature should remain unchanged. We decompose each observation into covered and residual components:
%
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \omega^i_t
    =
    \omega^{d,i}_t \oplus \omega^{u,i}_t,
    \qquad
    \mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}^d \cup \mathcal{F}^u,
    \qquad
    \mathcal{F}^d \cap \mathcal{F}^u = \emptyset ,
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:covered_residual_decomposition}
\end{equation}
%
where $\oplus$ denotes feature concatenation, $\omega^{d,i}_t$ contains symbolically predictable features, and $\omega^{u,i}_t$ contains residual features left to neural prediction. Consequently, an observation consists of two subparts:
\begin{enumerate*}[label={\roman*) },itemjoin={; \quad}]
  \item $\omega^{d,i}_t$: the symbolically covered sub-vector.
  \item $\omega^{u,i}_t$: the residual sub-vector.
\end{enumerate*}

\paragraph{Atomic symbolic rules.} We define an atomic symbolic transition rule $r_\ell \in \mathcal{R}$ as a partial function:
\begin{equation}
  r_\ell : H^j \times \Omega^j \times A^j \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}_\ell,
  \qquad
  r_\ell(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t)=f^d_{t+1,\ell},
  \label{eq:atomic_rule}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{Y}_\ell$ is the value space of covered feature $\ell$ and $1\leq \ell \leq |\mathcal{F}^d|$. This expresses how a covered feature evolves under symbolic transition knowledge.

\paragraph{Partial symbolic transition.} By aggregating all symbolic rules, we define a partial symbolic transition operator:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t) = \omega^{d,j}_{t+1},
  \label{eq:symbolic_transition}
\end{equation}
where $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1}$ is the predicted joint observation at time $t+1$ restricted to symbolically covered features for all agents.
%
We define a binary coverage mask $M_d \in \{0,1\}^{n \times n_f}$ indicating which agent-feature positions are produced by symbolic rules. Equivalently, let $P_d:\Omega^j\rightarrow\Omega^{d,j}$ and $P_u:\Omega^j\rightarrow\Omega^{u,j}$ select covered and residual components, and let $\iota_d:\Omega^{d,j}\rightarrow\Omega^j$ and $\iota_u:\Omega^{u,j}\rightarrow\Omega^j$ insert those components back into the full joint-observation space. These operators satisfy
\begin{equation}
  \omega^j_t = \iota_d(P_d\omega^j_t) + \iota_u(P_u\omega^j_t),
  \label{eq:selection_insertion}
\end{equation}
with disjoint covered and residual positions. The coverage ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \kappa = \frac{\|M_d\|_0}{n n_f},
  \label{eq:coverage_ratio}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa=1$ would correspond to a complete symbolic transition model and $\kappa=0$ to a purely neural WM. NS-MAWM is designed for the realistic intermediate regime $0 < \kappa < 1$.
This unified formalism underpins all subsequent neuro-symbolic integration strategies by enabling partial symbolic prediction while leaving uncovered components to the neural dynamics model.

\paragraph{Rule specification versus reward engineering.}
Symbolic transition rules introduce a domain-modeling burden, but they are not reward shaping terms. A reward specifies what behavior should be preferred; a transition rule specifies what next observations are plausible under the environment dynamics. Typical rules enforce constraints such as no two agents occupying the same cell, unavailable actions producing no active transition effect, dead or cooldown agents remaining inactive, bounded health/resource variables staying within valid ranges, objects persisting rather than duplicating or disappearing without interaction, and motion remaining within local kinematic limits. These rules do not say which action is good; they say which predicted successor states are admissible. This distinction makes them closer to structural transition priors than to behavioral objectives or policy constraints, but rule authoring can still be incomplete or wrong; we therefore report coverage, correction magnitude, and rule-dropout robustness rather than assuming perfect symbolic knowledge.

\subsection{Automating Partial Symbolic Coverage (Preliminary Study)}
\label{sec:llm_rules}

NS-MAWM does not require rules to come from a particular source. The main experiments use audited symbolic rules to isolate the effect of the covered/residual decomposition, but the same interface can also consume candidate rules produced by weaker or partially automated sources. We therefore include a preliminary study of LLM-assisted rule acquisition as a secondary experimental dimension.

The pipeline is deliberately simple. We provide an LLM with a compact natural-language environment description, including the observation features, action space, and examples of admissible and inadmissible transitions. The model is then asked to produce candidate transition rules in a structured textual format. These candidates are parsed into predicates over current observations, actions, and next covered features, then mapped to the NS-MAWM coverage mask whenever their referenced features are available. The resulting rule set is incomplete by design: generated rules may omit relevant constraints, duplicate existing constraints, use overly broad preconditions, or produce inconsistent effects.

We evaluate two exploratory conditions. \textbf{LLM-Raw} uses the parsed LLM-generated rules directly after syntactic validation. \textbf{LLM-Refined} applies lightweight manual filtering to remove rules that reference unavailable features, contradict the environment specification, or produce ambiguous next-feature assignments. The purpose is not to propose LLM rule generation as a new method, but to test whether the NS-MAWM interface can tolerate imperfect partial coverage. When rules are noisy, soft regularization can absorb them as a weak bias, while hard projection and residual dynamics must be interpreted with the same safeguards used elsewhere: rule coverage, RVR$_{pre}$, RVR$_{post}$, correction magnitude, and downstream planning metrics.


\subsection{Neuro-Symbolic Integration Strategies}
\label{sec:strategies}

We instantiate the covered/residual interface through three transition-level coupling modes. They form a design spectrum induced by the decomposition itself: regularization learns structure softly, projection enforces structure at rollout time, and residual dynamics factors covered structure out of the neural prediction target. All modes operate at the boundary where the WM predicts the next joint observation, so they do not require a particular neural backbone: the same interface can wrap an explicit structured predictor or a latent WM whose symbolic predicates are decoded, supervised, or otherwise exposed for rule checking.

Let
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}
  =
  \mathcal{T}^j_{\theta}(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t)
  \label{eq:raw_neural_prediction}
\end{equation}
be the raw neural prediction of the full next joint observation, and let
$\omega^{d,j}_{t+1} = \mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t)$
be the partial symbolic prediction on covered features.

\paragraph{Intuition and trade-offs.}
The three modes are coherent points in a single design space, not interchangeable engineering variants.
Regularization treats covered structure as a soft learning signal: it preserves the full neural prediction target and can improve learned semantic coherence, but it provides no inference-time guarantee.
Projection treats covered structure as a hard validity constraint: it gives the clearest covered-feature guarantee at rollout time, but large correction magnitudes would indicate that validity is being obtained by overwriting poor raw predictions.
Residual symbolic dynamics treats covered structure as a factorization of the prediction target: deterministic covered components are assigned to rules, and the neural model learns only residual uncertainty.
This can reduce the neural hypothesis space, but depends most strongly on a clean covered/residual split.
The evaluation uses these trade-offs directly: RVR$_{pre}$ tests learned structure, RVR$_{post}$ tests enforced validity, correction magnitude tests whether enforcement is local or repair-heavy, and planning return tests whether the resulting rollouts change decisions.

\subsubsection{Symbolic Consistency Regularization}
\label{sec:symbolic_loss_regularization}

This strategy, shown in the left panel of \autoref{fig:ns-mawm_overview}, adds symbolic supervision as a soft constraint: violations are penalized during training, but they are not impossible at inference time. It enforces nothing hard; it helps when symbolic rules provide a useful training signal; its limitation is that rollout-time validity remains empirical. The idea is to encourage the neural model (JOPM) to produce outputs $\hat{\omega}^{reg,j}_{t+1}$ that align with symbolic predictions where such knowledge is available. This is inspired by auxiliary loss strategies in hybrid models~\cite{Xu2018semanticloss, garcez2023neurosymbolic}. Let $P_d\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}$ be the covered part of the raw neural prediction and $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1}$ be the symbolic counterpart.
%
The symbolic consistency loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{symb}} = \| P_d\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1} - \omega^{d,j}_{t+1} \|^2,
  \label{eq:symbolic_loss}
\end{equation}
which is added to the main predictive loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{symb}},
  \label{eq:total_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is a regularization coefficient. This approach nudges the model to learn symbolically consistent dynamics in a differentiable way.

\subsubsection{Symbolic Projection}
\label{sec:symbolic_projection}

This strategy, shown in the center panel of \autoref{fig:ns-mawm_overview}, uses symbolic knowledge to override parts of the neural prediction. It enforces hard validity on covered features; it helps when invalid covered states mislead rollouts or planning; its limitation is that large corrections can hide poor raw neural predictions. This follows a common intervention approach in modular hybrid systems~\cite{garnelo2016deepsymbolicreinforcementlearning}. Instead of only encouraging consistency, we enforce it by projecting symbolically predictable features from the symbolic model:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\omega}^{proj,j}_{t+1}
  =
  \iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})
  + \iota_u(P_u\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}).
  \label{eq:symbolic_projection}
\end{equation}

The projected output $\hat{\omega}^{proj,j}_{t+1}$ is guaranteed to respect all symbolic constraints on the covered features, under correct rules and masks. It can be used as:
\begin{enumerate*}[label={\roman*) },itemjoin={; \quad}]
  \item Input to downstream planning.
  \item A target for retraining the WM (bootstrapping symbolic compliance).
  \item An explicit correction layer to improve rollout fidelity.
\end{enumerate*}

This approach guarantees zero covered-feature violations, but not necessarily small prediction error on uncovered features. The correction magnitude $\Delta_{\mathrm{corr}}$ is therefore reported to distinguish small repairs from large symbolic overwrites.

\subsubsection{Residual Symbolic Dynamics}
\label{sec:residual_symbolic_dynamics}

Here, as shown in the right panel of \autoref{fig:ns-mawm_overview}, we explicitly separate symbolic and neural prediction. It enforces covered-feature validity by construction and changes the neural learning problem; it helps when rules cover a stable deterministic core; its limitation is that a wrong or overly narrow mask can remove useful predictive targets from the neural model. The neural predictor is trained \emph{only} on residual features that are not covered by symbolic transition knowledge. This is a distinct residual-output prediction head, because its target space is $\Omega^{u,j}$ rather than the full observation space $\Omega^j$. Formally,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{T}^{u,j}_{\theta}: H^j \times \Omega^{u,j} \times A^j \rightarrow \Omega^{u,j}.
  \label{eq:residual_predictor_type}
\end{equation}
This architectural partitioning is inspired by residual learning frameworks in neuro-symbolic reasoning~\cite{garcez2023neurosymbolic, balloch2023neurosymbolicworldmodelsadapting}. The symbolic model computes:
\begin{equation}
  \omega^{d,j}_{t+1} = \mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t),
\end{equation}
while the neural model $\mathcal{T}_{\theta}^{u,j}$ is trained to predict:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1} = \mathcal{T}_{\theta}^{u,j}(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^{u,j}_t, a^j_t).
  \label{eq:residual_prediction}
\end{equation}

The final prediction is assembled by:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\omega}^{res,j}_{t+1}
  =
  \iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})
  +
  \iota_u(\hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1}).
  \label{eq:residual_assembly}
\end{equation}

This strategy reduces model complexity and encourages specialization: symbolic rules govern deterministic patterns, while neural components capture uncovered, stochastic, or high-entropy transitions. It is particularly advantageous when the symbolic rules cover a stable core of the environment while other dynamics remain residual.

\subsection{Post-Prediction Symbolic Correction}
\label{sec:post_prediction_correction}

We use symbolic post-correction as an ablation and diagnostic mechanism rather than as an additional contribution. It is most useful for the purely neural MAWM baseline and for the regularized variant, where the raw prediction can still violate symbolic rules at inference time. For projection and residual dynamics, covered features are already enforced by construction, so post-correction is redundant on $\mathcal{F}^d$ except as a way to measure correction magnitude.

The correction operator enforces symbolic consistency
on a neural prediction at inference time, without retraining:
%
$\tilde{\omega}^{j}_{t+1} = \mathcal{C}^{s}(\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1})$,
%
where $\mathcal{C}^{s}$ applies domain-specific symbolic constraints
(such as mutual exclusivity, valid state transitions, or discretization of categorical features).
The operator may be inexpensive in grid-like domains, but can become non-trivial when multiple constraints conflict; we therefore report correction magnitude and runtime overhead rather than assuming correction is always negligible.

\subsection{Algorithmic Summary}
\label{sec:algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{NS-MAWM training and rollout inference}
  \label{alg:ns_mawm}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \REQUIRE replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, rule set $\mathcal{R}$, integration mode $m \in \{\mathrm{reg},\mathrm{proj},\mathrm{res}\}$, coverage mask $M_d$
    \STATE Build the partial symbolic transition operator $\mathcal{T}^j_s$ from $\mathcal{R}$ and compute covered/residual feature sets $(\mathcal{F}^d,\mathcal{F}^u)$.
    \STATE Initialize neural dynamics $\mathcal{T}^j_\theta$ for full prediction if $m\in\{\mathrm{reg},\mathrm{proj}\}$, or residual dynamics $\mathcal{T}^{u,j}_\theta$ if $m=\mathrm{res}$.
    \FOR{each minibatch $(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t,\omega^j_{t+1})$ from $\mathcal{D}$}
    \STATE Compute symbolic prediction $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1}=\mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)$.
    \IF{$m=\mathrm{reg}$}
    \STATE Predict $\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}=\mathcal{T}^j_\theta(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)$ and optimize $\mathcal{L}_{pred}+\lambda\mathcal{L}_{symb}$.
    \ELSIF{$m=\mathrm{proj}$}
    \STATE Train $\mathcal{T}^j_\theta$ with $\mathcal{L}_{pred}$; at rollout time set $\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1}=\iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})+\iota_u(P_u\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1})$.
    \ELSE
    \STATE Train $\mathcal{T}^{u,j}_\theta$ to predict $\hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1}$ and assemble $\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1}=\iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})+\iota_u(\hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1})$.
    \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE During evaluation, generate open-loop rollouts, decode categorical groups by argmax, and report MSE, RVR$_{pre}$, RVR$_{post}$, correction magnitude, and downstream metrics.
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Rule Violation Rate}
\label{sec:rvr}

We use \textbf{Rule Violation Rate} (RVR) as a transition-level diagnostic lens for semantic reliability in structured environments. Prediction accuracy and semantic validity are distinct properties: a model can be close in MSE while predicting an inadmissible transition, and a post-projection rollout can be valid while hiding a poor raw neural prediction. RVR measures how often model predictions violate symbolic transition rules on covered features (\autoref{sec:ns}); its pre/post variants distinguish learned semantic coherence from validity obtained by enforcement.

Given the symbolic prediction
$\omega^{d,j}_{t+1} = \mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1}, \omega^j_t, a^j_t)$
and an evaluated model prediction $\hat{\omega}^{eval,j}_{t+1}$, where
$eval\in\{pre,post\}$ for RVR$_{pre}$ or RVR$_{post}$ respectively, we define the violation indicator:
\begin{equation}
  v_{i,k,t} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } \left| (P_d\hat{\omega}^{eval,j}_{t+1})_{i,k} - (\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})_{i,k} \right| > \epsilon, \\
    0 & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:rvr_indicator}
\end{equation}
where $(i,k)$ indexes agents and symbolically covered features, and $\epsilon$ is a tolerance threshold.
%
The \textbf{RVR} over $T$ transitions is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{RVR} =
  \frac{1}{n T |\mathcal{F}^d|}
  \sum_{t=1}^T \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{k \in \mathcal{F}^d} v_{i,k,t}.
  \label{eq:rvr}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Pre- and post-correction RVR.}
To avoid conflating model quality with enforced validity, we distinguish two quantities. $\mathrm{RVR}_{pre}$ is computed on the raw neural prediction before projection, residual assembly, or symbolic correction; it measures how much semantic structure the learned component has internalized. $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}$ is computed on the final output used for rollout or planning; it measures whether the generated trajectory satisfies covered constraints after the chosen coupling mode. For projection and residual symbolic dynamics, $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}=0$ on $\mathcal{F}^d$ by construction under correct rules and masks, whereas $\mathrm{RVR}_{pre}$ may remain nonzero. Reporting only $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}$ would therefore be misleading for hard modes: projection can force covered features onto the symbolic manifold even when the raw prediction is semantically poor. The gap between pre- and post-correction RVR is diagnostic: small gaps suggest learned or locally enforced coherence, while large gaps indicate repair-heavy validity that may not reflect a reliable WM.

\paragraph{Statistical interpretation.}
Each $v_{i,k,t}$ is a Bernoulli violation indicator under the rollout distribution induced by the evaluated model. RVR is its empirical mean over covered agent-feature-time triples, and its expectation estimates the covered-feature violation probability under that evaluation distribution. It is not a distribution-free certificate of global model correctness and it is not directly comparable with downstream return. We therefore report RVR as mean $\pm$ standard error across independent seeds, and interpret close differences only with paired comparisons over matched seeds and environments.

\paragraph{Correction magnitude.}
For hard integration methods, RVR must be read together with the amount of symbolic intervention. We therefore also report how much symbolic correction is applied:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_{\mathrm{corr}} =
  \frac{1}{T}
  \sum_{t=1}^{T}
  \left\|
  P_d\left(\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1} - \hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}\right)
  \right\|_2 .
  \label{eq:correction_magnitude}
\end{equation}
This metric prevents a misleading interpretation of zero post-correction RVR. A method that achieves zero violations by making very large corrections is different from one whose neural predictions are already close to the symbolic manifold. RVR$_{pre}$, RVR$_{post}$, and $\Delta_{\mathrm{corr}}$ are therefore reported together: they separate statistical accuracy from semantic validity, learned validity from enforced validity, and local correction from large repair.

\subsection{Formal Properties and Scope of Guarantees}
\label{sec:formal_properties}

NS-MAWM provides conditional guarantees on the symbolically covered part of the prediction, not universal guarantees on full task performance.

\begin{proposition}[Covered-feature validity]
  Assume that the symbolic transition operator $\mathcal{T}^j_s$ is correct on $\mathcal{F}^d$ and that the coverage mask $M_d$ correctly identifies the covered feature positions. Then symbolic projection and residual symbolic dynamics produce final predictions satisfying all symbolic transition rules on $\mathcal{F}^d$ at each predicted step.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  In symbolic projection, the final prediction is
  $\iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})+\iota_u(P_u\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1})$,
  so applying $P_d$ recovers $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1}=\mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)$.
  In residual symbolic dynamics, the final prediction is
  $\iota_d(\omega^{d,j}_{t+1})+\iota_u(\hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1})$,
  so the same covered component is recovered. Therefore, under the stated correctness assumptions, final covered features match the symbolic transition output, and no covered-feature rule violation remains.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
  The proposition does not imply that all features are correct, that uncovered residual dynamics are accurately predicted, or that downstream return always improves. Claims about long-horizon prediction, planning/control, robustness, and generalization are empirical claims tested in \autoref{sec:eval}.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Covered/residual error decomposition]
  Let $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1,\star}=P_d\omega^j_{t+1,\star}$ denote the true covered component and
  $\omega^{u,j}_{t+1,\star}=P_u\omega^j_{t+1,\star}$ the true residual component. For projection or
  residual symbolic dynamics, the squared prediction error decomposes as
  \begin{equation}
    \|\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1}-\omega^j_{t+1,\star}\|_2^2
    =
    \|\mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)-\omega^{d,j}_{t+1,\star}\|_2^2
    +
    \|P_u\hat{\omega}^{post,j}_{t+1}-\omega^{u,j}_{t+1,\star}\|_2^2 .
    \label{eq:error_decomposition}
  \end{equation}
  If the symbolic rules are correct on $\mathcal{F}^d$, the first term is zero; the remaining error is entirely residual.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Projection and residual symbolic dynamics both set the covered component of
  the final prediction to $\mathcal{T}^j_s(h^j_{t-1},\omega^j_t,a^j_t)$. The
  uncovered component is $P_u\hat{\omega}^{pre,j}_{t+1}$ for projection and
  $\hat{\omega}^{u,j}_{t+1}$ for residual symbolic dynamics. Since covered and
  residual feature positions are disjoint, the squared Euclidean error separates
  into the two stated terms. Correct symbolic rules make the covered-feature
  term zero.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
  This decomposition explains the precise theoretical role of symbolic
  knowledge: it can remove error on correctly covered features, but it cannot
  by itself bound residual-feature error, convergence, or downstream return.
  Those claims require the empirical tests below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Design Rationale and Gap Alignment}
\label{sec:design_rationale}

NS-MAWM is designed to align formal structure, integration mechanism, and empirical validation. The covered/residual decomposition addresses \textbf{G1} by identifying where symbolic knowledge can be meaningfully applied under partial observability. The three integration modes address \textbf{G2}: regularization tests whether symbolic knowledge can be learned as a soft bias, projection tests hard enforcement at inference, and residual dynamics tests whether removing covered features from the neural target reduces the burden on the learned model. The evaluation design addresses \textbf{G3} by separating formal validity on covered features from empirical questions about prediction error, correction magnitude, downstream return, rule dropout, OOD generalization, and data-limited learning.

The empirical protocol follows directly from this alignment. RVR$_{pre}$ measures learned semantic coherence, RVR$_{post}$ measures final covered-feature validity, $\Delta_{\mathrm{corr}}$ measures the size of symbolic intervention, and downstream return tests whether more reliable imagined trajectories change model-based action selection. These quantities are reported together because any one of them alone can be misleading: zero RVR$_{post}$ can be achieved by large repairs, low prediction error can hide invalid transitions, and semantic validity matters for control only if it affects planning under a matched decision budget.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

The empirical evaluation tests the claims that cannot be established deductively from the framework alone. This section specifies how the formal claims above are turned into empirical tests by describing the shared neural backbone, symbolic rule interface, benchmark environments, reporting protocol, and ablations before interpreting quantitative results. We organize the evaluation around seven validation questions:
\begin{enumerate*}[label={\textbf{EQ\arabic*}.}, itemjoin={\quad}]
  \item does NS-MAWM improve one-step and long-horizon predictive fidelity under matched budgets?;
  \item does it reduce learned and post-correction symbolic violations?;
  \item does it reduce long-horizon semantic drift?;
  \item do more reliable rollouts improve downstream planning/control?;
  \item does performance degrade gracefully under partial symbolic coverage?;
  \item does the framework generalize to unseen multi-agent configurations?;
  \item does symbolic structure improve learning in data-limited regimes?
\end{enumerate*}
These questions provide inductive evidence for the theoretical gaps in \autoref{sec:intro}, while \autoref{sec:formal_properties} states the limited deductive guarantee that holds on covered features.

\paragraph{Experimental logic.}
The evaluation is organized by claim family rather than by benchmark alone. Prediction fidelity tests whether the covered/residual decomposition preserves or improves one-step and rollout accuracy. Semantic validity tests whether the model learns constraints before enforcement and satisfies covered constraints after enforcement. Decision relevance tests whether more reliable imagined trajectories improve model-based planning under the same planner budget. Robustness and generalization test whether the method degrades gracefully as symbolic coverage becomes partial, noisy, or out-of-distribution. A claim is considered supported only when its main metric, baseline comparison, and per-environment or per-horizon analysis agree; aggregate tables are used for orientation, not as standalone proof.

\subsection{Implementation}
\label{sec:impl_overview}

\footnotetext[1]{\label{fn:github}
  An anonymized implementation of NS-MAWM and full experimental details
  (including hyperparameters, architectures, and rule definitions)
  are available at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/NS-MAWM-83D8/README.md}.}

\paragraph{WM backbone.}
\textit{NS-MAWM}~\hyperref[fn:github]{\footnotemark[1]} builds on a shared MAWM backbone
(\autoref{sec:mawm_basics}) and is implemented in \texttt{PyTorch}~\cite{paszke2019pytorch}.
We use a centralized JOPM comprising
(i) a shared encoder $Enc^j$ over joint observations,
(ii) an LSTM-based latent dynamics core~\cite{hochreiter1997long},
and (iii) a decoder $Dec^j$ predicting the next joint observation.
Joint observations are concatenated along the agent dimension and embedded using MLPs with ReLU activations.
Discrete structured features are represented as one-hot vectors and treated as continuous outputs,
while continuous features are predicted directly.
At evaluation time, categorical features are decoded by an argmax over each one-hot group before rule checking; continuous rule predicates use the tolerance $\epsilon$ defined for RVR.
The prediction loss is
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{F}_{\text{cont}}}\|\hat{f}_{k}-f_{k}\|^2$.
All neural components are optimized using Adam~\cite{kingma2017adammethodstochasticoptimization}
with a fixed learning rate.

\paragraph{Neuro-symbolic components.}
The symbolic transition operator $\mathcal{T}^j_s$ (\autoref{sec:ns}) is implemented as a Python rule engine.
Each rule deterministically produces a subset of symbolically covered features $\omega^{d,j}_{t+1}$
and a corresponding coverage mask $M_d$.
This modular design supports selective rule activation for ablation and robustness studies.
We evaluate three integration strategies (\autoref{sec:strategies}):
(i) symbolic consistency regularization via an auxiliary loss inspired by semantic loss~\cite{Xu2018semanticloss,Fan2017differentiablelearninglogicalrules},
(ii) symbolic projection at inference time,
and (iii) residual symbolic dynamics, restricting neural prediction to residual features.
We further evaluate an inference-time symbolic correction operator $\mathcal{C}^s$
(\autoref{sec:post_prediction_correction}),
which enforces validity constraints (such as mutual exclusivity or spatial feasibility)
using simple projection and discretization operators~\cite{garcez2023neurosymbolic}.

\paragraph{Training data and interfaces.}
WMs are trained offline from replay buffers collected using random or weakly exploratory policies.
We rely on standard MARL interfaces:
\texttt{PettingZoo}~\cite{terry2020pettingzoo},
\texttt{Overcooked-AI}~\cite{Micah2020overcooked},
and the \texttt{SMACv2} benchmark~\cite{Ellis2023smacv2}.
The released environment file pins the tested Python, NumPy, Gym/Gymnasium,
PettingZoo, Overcooked-AI, and SMACv2 versions. In particular, the Overcooked
setup uses either a Gymnasium-compatible wrapper or a pinned legacy Gym/NumPy
combination, because unpinned Gym installations can fail with NumPy 2.x.
Replay buffers store joint observations, actions, and next observations.
To assess data frugality, models are trained on multiple dataset sizes
($N_{\text{small}}=10^4$, $N_{\text{med}}=5\times 10^4$, and $N_{\text{full}}=2\times 10^5$ transitions),
while keeping architectures and training schedules fixed across methods.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter selection.}
Neural hyperparameters (learning rate, latent dimension, LSTM size, depth)
are selected automatically using Optuna~\cite{akiba2019optuna}
by minimizing validation prediction loss.
Hyperparameter optimization is applied exclusively to neural components
and independently of symbolic rules, with identical search spaces and budgets across methods.
Selected hyperparameters are fixed for all subsequent experiments.


\subsection{Environments and Rule Sets}
\label{sec:envs}

We consider four representative multi-agent environments:

\textbf{(E1) Gridcraft~\hyperref[fn:gridcraft]{\footnotemark[2]}, a Minecraft-like gridworld (discrete, partially observable).}
A grid-based environment with $n$ agents, walls/obstacles, and typed objects.
Observations are structured features (relative positions, occupancy, object presence),
making invariants and equivariances explicit.
Rules~\hyperref[fn:github]{\footnotemark[1]} include collision avoidance, boundary constraints, object persistence, and action-conditioned motion equivariances.
Gridcraft is the controlled testbed for rule coverage, symbolic mask correctness, and long-horizon semantic drift because its relevant invariants can be audited feature by feature.

\footnotetext[2]{\label{fn:gridcraft}An anonymized version is available at: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Gridcraft-006A/README.md}.}

\paragraph{(E2) Overcooked ~\cite{Micah2020overcooked} (discrete, cooperative coordination).}
We use Overcooked layouts featuring sparse rewards and strong coordination requirements.
Rules~\hyperref[fn:github]{\footnotemark[1]} capture state-transition constraints (pickup/drop interactions), mutually exclusive object states,
and spatial movement constraints.
Overcooked tests whether semantic validity remains useful when coordination, partner behavior, and task timing make the residual dynamics nontrivial.

\paragraph{(E3) Predator--Prey~\cite{lowe2017multi} (continuous or mixed, multi-agent interaction).}
We use a standard Predator--Prey benchmark with continuous positions/velocities (or mixed discrete actions).
Rules~\hyperref[fn:github]{\footnotemark[1]} encode kinematic constraints (bounded velocity), proximity-based capture conditions, and persistence.
Predator--Prey tests multi-agent interaction dynamics in which symbolic constraints cover feasibility and capture predicates, while pursuit and evasion remain residual.

\paragraph{(E4) SMACv2~\cite{Ellis2023smacv2} (StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge v2, partially observable).}
We evaluate on a subset of SMACv2 scenarios with varying team sizes.
Rules~\hyperref[fn:github]{\footnotemark[1]} focus on movement feasibility and action-conditioned invariances
(such as if an agent does not move, its position remains consistent; cooldown-like constraints).
SMACv2 is the stress test for scale, partial observability, and scenario shift; it is not expected to be fully symbolically covered.

\paragraph{Rule coverage.}
For each environment, the rule set $\mathcal{R}$ implements the partial symbolic transition operator $\mathcal{T}^j_s$ and its mask $M_d$. We report the number of rules, the covered feature families, representative examples, and the coverage ratio $\kappa=\|M_d\|_0/(n n_f)$. This is essential for interpreting both guarantees and empirical results: NS-MAWM only guarantees covered-feature validity on $\mathcal{F}^d$, while uncovered features remain governed by the neural model.

\paragraph{Rule complexity and scalability.}
The symbolic rules used here are local transition rules, not a full simulator: each rule reads a bounded subset of agent, object, or action features and writes a bounded subset of covered next-observation features. Representative examples include ``a blocked movement preserves position'', ``two exclusive object states cannot both be active'', ``an unavailable action cannot produce its corresponding state transition'', ``an inactive agent cannot trigger attack or movement effects'', and ``inventory or health variables remain within admissible bounds unless a matching event occurs''. If $|\mathcal{R}|$ is the number of active rules and each rule touches at most $c$ feature entries per agent, a direct implementation has cost $O(n|\mathcal{R}|c)$ per rollout step before vectorization. This scaling is acceptable for the benchmark regimes studied here, but it motivates reporting the number of rules and coverage ratio per environment rather than presenting symbolic reasoning as free.

\subsection{Evaluation Axes and Baselines}
\label{sec:eval_axes_baselines}

The evaluation is structured around three systematic axes that test specific facets of the decomposition hypothesis. Rather than comparing a fixed set of isolated baselines, we vary one axis at a time while holding others fixed, enabling clear interpretation of what drives observed improvements.

\paragraph{Axis 1: Symbolic coverage ($\kappa$).}
Symbolic coverage measures the fraction of observation features that are governed by explicit transition rules:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No coverage ($\kappa=0$):} Pure neural multi-agent world model (MAWM) with no symbolic rules. This is the primary baseline for decomposition; it represents the joint learning paradigm.
  \item \textbf{Partial coverage ($\kappa \in [0.18, 0.42]$):} Manually specified rules covering stable transition structure (spatial feasibility, collision exclusivity, resource conservation, action availability). This is the main operating regime for NS-MAWM.
  \item \textbf{LLM-generated partial coverage:} Automatically generated rules with lower precision; tests graceful degradation under noisy symbolic knowledge.
  \item \textbf{Near-complete coverage (oracle):} Ground-truth symbolic transition on all covered features; tests the upper bound of what symbolic structure alone can achieve.
\end{itemize}

The coverage ratio $\kappa$ makes explicit the boundary between what the rules can guarantee and what remains a learned residual problem. NS-MAWM's hypothesis is not that symbolic rules solve the entire prediction problem, but that separating covered structure reduces model burden and improves semantic reliability on the residual component.

\paragraph{Axis 2: Integration regime.}
Three mechanisms couple symbolic and neural components, each answering a different diagnostic question:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Regularization (soft coupling during training):} An auxiliary symbolic consistency loss encourages the neural predictor to learn rule-conforming patterns. This tests whether semantic structure can be internalized as a learned inductive bias. RVR$_{\text{pre}}$ measures success; RVR$_{\text{post}}$ equals RVR$_{\text{pre}}$ since no hard enforcement occurs. Expected behavior: reduced learned violations, but no guarantee of zero violations over long horizons.
  \item \textbf{Projection (hard coupling at inference):} Covered-feature predictions are overwritten by symbolic rules after neural decoding. This tests whether hard correction is useful without changing the target representation during training. RVR$_{\text{post}}=0$ on covered features by construction; correction magnitude measures how much repair was needed. Expected behavior: zero post-correction violations, smaller corrections than post-hoc repair, preserved residual MSE.
  \item \textbf{Residual symbolic dynamics (strong factorization):} Covered features are removed from the neural target and assigned to the symbolic operator; only residual features are predicted neurally. This tests whether factorizing the prediction target reduces the neural model's burden. No RVR$_{\text{pre}}$ is reported since covered features are not predicted; RVR$_{\text{post}}=0$ by construction. Expected behavior: lowest MSE across regimes, fastest learning in data-limited settings, zero covered-feature violations without explicit correction.
\end{itemize}

These regimes span a design space induced by the covered/residual decomposition. They should not be read as competing variants that differ only in aggregate score, but as distinct coupling mechanisms that offer different trade-offs between enforcement strength, modification scope, and interpretability.

\paragraph{Axis 3: World model backbone.}
The neural component is held constant across symbolic variants to isolate the effect of decomposition:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Shared MAWM backbone:} A centralized joint-observation encoder, LSTM-based latent dynamics, and multi-agent structured decoder. All symbolic and neural baselines use identical hyperparameters, search budgets, and training schedules.
  \item \textbf{RSSM/Dreamer-style latent WM:} A standard recurrent latent-variable world model from the MBRL literature. Included to test whether stronger standard WM architectures remove the need for explicit semantic constraints.
  \item \textbf{Structured rule-feature baseline:} Symbolic predicates are exposed as inputs to the neural model without enforcing consistency. Tests whether feature access alone, independent of coupling mechanism, explains gains.
\end{itemize}

Keeping the backbone fixed ensures that observed improvements come from the covered/residual coupling mechanism rather than from increased capacity, different learning rates, or incompatible architecture choices.

\subsection{Metrics, Baselines, and Protocol}
\label{sec:metrics_baselines_protocol}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare a purely neural multi-agent world model
(\textbf{MAWM (Neural)}) against three NS-MAWM variants:
(i) \textbf{symbolic consistency regularization},
(ii) \textbf{symbolic projection},
and (iii) \textbf{residual symbolic dynamics}.
All methods share the same neural backbone and training budget.
Unless stated otherwise, symbolic post-correction $\mathcal{C}^s$ is disabled.
For context against standard model-based RL architectures, we also include a recurrent latent WM baseline in the RSSM/Dreamer family and a structured rule-feature baseline that exposes symbolic predicates to the neural predictor without enforcing them.
The baseline set isolates the main alternative explanations for positive results: access to symbolic features alone, post-hoc repair alone, soft regularization alone, hard projection alone, a stronger latent-state WM backbone, an oracle-like advantage from complete symbolic simulation, and the effect of imperfect automatically generated rules.

\paragraph{Ablation grid.}
The minimal ablation grid removes one mechanism at a time: no symbolic regularization, no symbolic projection, no covered/residual split, post-correction only, symbolic features only, rule dropout, noisy or incomplete rules, LLM-generated rules, horizon sweeps, and data-limited replay subsets. These ablations are interpreted relative to the same neural backbone and dataset splits, so a positive result cannot be attributed merely to more parameters, more inputs, or a different planner budget.

\paragraph{Exploratory LLM-rule protocol.}
The LLM-rule study is evaluated with the same metrics as the audited-rule experiments, but its interpretation is deliberately narrower. LLM-Raw and LLM-Refined are not intended to be competitive rule-generation methods; they probe whether NS-MAWM can use imperfect symbolic coverage produced without full manual rule design. We therefore report their rule coverage, rejected-rule rate, RVR$_{pre}$, RVR$_{post}$, correction magnitude, and planning metrics alongside the audited-rule variants. A useful outcome is not perfect validity, but graceful degradation: automatically generated rules should improve over no rules only when their coverage is informative, and refinement should reduce harmful or inconsistent constraints.

\paragraph{Predictive fidelity.}
Prediction accuracy is evaluated at one step and over open-loop rollouts
of horizon $H=25$, where model predictions are fed back as inputs.
We report the aggregated multi-step error
$
  \text{MSE}_H =
  \frac{1}{H}
  \sum_{t=1}^{H}
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[
    \|\hat{\omega}^j_t - \omega^j_t\|_2^2
    \right]
$,
averaged over trajectories.

\paragraph{Symbolic coherence.}
Symbolic consistency is measured using the
\textbf{RVR} (\autoref{sec:rvr})
on open-loop rollouts, which quantifies violations of symbolically covered features
$\mathcal{F}^d$.
For projection and residual strategies, covered features are enforced
by construction, yielding $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}=0$ under correct rules and masks.
We therefore report both $\mathrm{RVR}_{pre}$ and $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}$ when evaluating learned versus enforced coherence.

\paragraph{Downstream planning/control.}
To test decision relevance, we use learned WMs inside a shared receding-horizon model-based planner. For discrete domains, the planner samples $K$ candidate joint action sequences of horizon $H_p$, scores them with the learned WM and a shared reward model, executes only the first joint action, and replans at the next state. For mixed or larger action spaces, we use the same interface with a CEM-style proposal distribution over joint action sequences. All WM variants use identical $H_p$, candidate budget $K$, reward model, action constraints, start-state distribution, and execution environment. Thus, differences in return, success rate, or invalid imagined plans are attributable to the world model used by the planner rather than to a different controller, reward interface, or search budget. This experiment does not require full RL retraining, but it directly tests whether semantic rollout reliability changes action selection under a matched planning budget.

\paragraph{Generalization and robustness.}
Generalization is evaluated on out-of-distribution configurations,
including unseen layouts, varying agent counts,
and alternative SMACv2 scenarios.
Robustness to incomplete symbolic knowledge is assessed
by randomly dropping a fraction $\rho \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75\}$
of symbolic rules during training and inference.

\paragraph{Robustness and sensitivity.}
We evaluate sensitivity to seeds, rollout horizons, symbolic coverage, rule dropout, noisy or incomplete rules when available, dataset size, OOD layouts/configurations, and planning budget. Robustness is not defined as invariant performance under all perturbations; it means controlled degradation as symbolic coverage decreases, no collapse when rules are partial, and preserved residual-feature prediction when covered features are enforced. Hard symbolic modes can hurt when rules or masks are wrong, so misspecified-rule experiments are interpreted as stress tests rather than expected improvements.

\paragraph{Protocol.}
For each environment, all methods are trained on the same offline dataset
and identical train/validation splits, and evaluated on held-out trajectories.
For data-limited experiments, replay subsets are nested so that
$N_{\mathrm{small}} \subset N_{\mathrm{med}} \subset N_{\mathrm{full}}$
under the same random seed.
Results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard error over 5 random seeds. The standard error is computed across independently seeded runs that resample initialization, replay-buffer collection, and training order.
Pairwise method comparisons against MAWM use paired tests over matched seeds and environments; effect sizes are reported alongside $p$-values whenever the qualitative conclusion depends on a close numerical difference.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\autoref{tab:results_overview} reports quantitative results across all environments and evaluation dimensions.
\autoref{tab:per_env_prediction}--\autoref{tab:data_limited} group the evidence by claim family: per-environment prediction, semantic drift, partial-rule robustness, planning/OOD behavior, and data-limited learning. We interpret aggregate improvements only when they are consistent with the corresponding per-environment, horizon-wise, and seed-level diagnostics.

\paragraph{Long-horizon prediction accuracy and error accumulation (G3).}
The aggregate table in \autoref{tab:results_overview} tests the central decomposition hypothesis: separating rule-covered structure from residual uncertainty should improve iterative rollout behavior when symbolic coverage is informative. \autoref{tab:error_accumulation} targets the associated failure mode of compounding error. Across environments, NS-MAWM variants reduce aggregate MSE$_H$ relative to MAWM, with the residual variant giving the strongest reduction ([33.6\%]) and the projection variant remaining competitive ([27.9\%]). The horizon sweep shows that the benefit is not limited to one-step prediction: the error-growth index decreases from [5.03 $\pm$ 0.39] for MAWM to [3.72 $\pm$ 0.25] for residual symbolic dynamics. This supports the narrower claim that partial symbolic structure can slow rollout degradation when rules cover stable transition components; it does not imply that residual features are solved.

\paragraph{Validation of the decomposition hypothesis.}
The evaluation collectively tests whether the covered/residual decomposition improves semantic reliability when symbolic coverage is informative. This hypothesis predicts a consistent pattern across axes:

\emph{Along the coverage axis,} moving from $\kappa=0$ (MAWM) to partial manual coverage (NS-MAWM variants) should reduce both learned violations (RVR$_{pre}$) and post-correction violations (RVR$_{post}$), slow error accumulation, and improve planning return. The oracle baseline (near-complete coverage) provides an upper bound; any remaining error on that bound is attributable to residual uncertainty, not symbolic knowledge gaps. Degradation under rule dropout ($\rho > 0$) should be controlled monotonic rather than precipitous, demonstrating that the interface between covered and residual components is robust to incomplete knowledge.

\emph{Along the regime axis,} integrating symbolic structure at different coupling strengths should reveal their trade-offs. Regularization achieves soft bias through learned internalization; projection achieves hard guarantees at modest correction cost; residual dynamics achieves both low error and hard guarantees by factorizing the prediction target. The relative ranking (regularization $<$ projection $<$ residual in MSE and error slope) should reflect this increasing structural commitment, and empirical gains should not exceed what the oracle baseline provides on covered features.

\emph{Along the backbone axis,} improvements should not depend on a specific neural architecture. The RSSM/Dreamer baseline represents a stronger recurrent latent model; if decomposition benefits were merely architectural, this baseline would close the gap. Instead, the structured difference (covered vs. residual) should matter across backbones, with the largest absolute gains on weaker neural baselines (lower capacity, smaller data regimes) where the burden reduction from decomposition is greatest.

\emph{Across axes,} the decomposition hypothesis is supported only when improvements are consistent with all three metrics (MSE, RVR, return), persist beyond nominal conditions (OOD, partial coverage, data-limited), and are explained by the separation of structure from uncertainty rather than by post-hoc repair alone. The correction-magnitude diagnostics are critical: hard modes should show low RVR$_{pre}$ (meaning the neural model learned structure) and low $\Delta_{\text{corr}}$ (meaning the predictions were already close to valid), not zero violations from large late-stage repairs that hide underlying prediction error.

Together, these per-axis results support the central claim: explicit decomposition of structured and residual dynamics improves both learned and enforced semantic validity, slows long-horizon error accumulation, and leads to more reliable model-based planning---without requiring complete symbolic coverage or perfect rule correctness.

\paragraph{Symbolic coherence and constraint satisfaction (G2, G3).}
RVR is used as a diagnostic framework rather than as a single success score. RVR$_{pre}$ asks whether the learned predictor has internalized semantic structure before any enforcement; RVR$_{post}$ asks whether the final rollout consumed by evaluation or planning satisfies the covered constraints; correction magnitude measures how much intervention was needed to obtain that final state. The neural baseline produces nonzero violations on covered features ([0.194 $\pm$ 0.021]), while symbolic regularization reduces learned violations to [0.071 $\pm$ 0.012] without eliminating them. Projection and residual symbolic dynamics yield $\mathrm{RVR}_{post}=0$ on covered features by construction under correct rules and masks; their empirical role is therefore to show whether this guarantee is useful without requiring large corrections or harming residual prediction. \autoref{tab:rvr_correction} makes this distinction explicit: post-correction also reaches zero RVR$_{post}$, but with larger corrections ([0.083 $\pm$ 0.011] median) than projection ([0.031 $\pm$ 0.006] median), indicating that NS-MAWM is not merely repairing arbitrary neural outputs at the end of a rollout. \autoref{tab:rvr_triggers} and \autoref{fig:rvr_diagnostic} further decompose corrections by trigger type and visually connect correction spikes to planner-relevant failure modes such as spatial infeasibility, object inconsistency, and unavailable actions. Across matched planning runs, invalid imagined plan rate is negatively associated with return (Spearman $\rho=[-0.78]$), and horizon-averaged RVR is positively associated with rollout error growth ($\rho=[0.74]$). We interpret these correlations as diagnostic evidence, not as proof that every semantic violation is equally decision-critical.

\paragraph{Downstream decision relevance (G3).}
\autoref{tab:planning_ood} and \autoref{fig:planning_utility} test whether semantic reliability changes actual decisions rather than only consistency metrics. The planner, reward interface, action constraints, candidate budget, start-state distribution, and execution environment are matched across methods; the learned WM is the controlled variable. Consequently, differences in return and invalid imagined plans reflect how each model ranks candidate action sequences under the same decision budget. Under this protocol, the residual variant improves return from [41.2 $\pm$ 3.8] to [57.6 $\pm$ 2.7], increases success rate from [0.48 $\pm$ 0.05] to [0.69 $\pm$ 0.04], and reduces invalid imagined plans from [0.27 $\pm$ 0.04] to [0.04 $\pm$ 0.01]. Projection shows the same qualitative direction, with slightly lower return but strong validity. These results support the measured claim that semantically cleaner rollouts can improve model-based decision quality under matched planning budgets when symbolic coverage is informative.

\paragraph{Robustness and data efficiency (G3).}
Rule-dropout experiments in \autoref{tab:rule_dropout} show controlled degradation rather than collapse as symbolic coverage is reduced. For example, normalized performance for residual symbolic dynamics decreases from [1.00 $\pm$ 0.00] at full coverage to [0.79 $\pm$ 0.05] when [75\%] of rules are dropped. Data-limited experiments in \autoref{tab:data_limited} show the largest relative gains in the smallest replay regime: residual symbolic dynamics reduces MSE from [0.178 $\pm$ 0.012] to [0.105 $\pm$ 0.008], a relative improvement of [41.0\%]. This is consistent with the hypothesis-space interpretation of the covered/residual decomposition: symbolic rules reduce the amount of deterministic structure that the neural component must relearn from data.

\paragraph{Preliminary LLM-generated rules.}
The exploratory LLM-rule study tests whether the covered/residual interface remains useful when symbolic coverage is automatically generated and imperfect. In the settings tested, LLM-Raw rules provide partial coverage that improves semantic diagnostics over the no-rule baseline when generated constraints match exposed transition features, but the gains are weaker and less stable than with audited symbolic rules. LLM-Refined improves over LLM-Raw by removing contradictory or feature-incompatible rules, reducing correction magnitude and invalid imagined plans. These results are preliminary: they do not establish LLM rule generation as a contribution, but they indicate that NS-MAWM can degrade gracefully when symbolic knowledge is acquired from a noisy source rather than fully specified by hand.

\paragraph{Comparing integration strategies.}
The three integration modes answer different diagnostic questions. Regularization tests whether soft symbolic pressure improves learned coherence, projection tests whether hard correction helps at inference time, and residual dynamics tests whether assigning covered features to symbolic rules reduces the neural model's burden. We therefore report not only aggregate MSE and RVR, but also correction magnitude, downstream return, rule-dropout degradation, and OOD behavior for each mode.

\paragraph{Statistical interpretation.}
Reported aggregate values are interpreted as support for a claim only when the improvement is consistent across matched seeds or environments and is not contradicted by the paired uncertainty analysis. When differences are small, the paper reports them as trends rather than definitive improvements.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Interpreting semantic validity.}
The central question is not whether symbolic rules can force covered features to satisfy themselves; projection and residual modes do that by construction under correct rules and masks. The substantive question is whether decomposing transition predictability improves learned WM behavior without hiding large corrections, harming residual prediction, or failing to matter for downstream decisions. NS-MAWM should therefore be judged jointly through MSE, RVR$_{pre}$, RVR$_{post}$, correction magnitude, downstream return, OOD behavior, and rule-dropout robustness.

\paragraph{Interpreting the three integration modes.}
The three modes should not be read as interchangeable variants that compete only on a single aggregate score. They span a principled design space induced by the covered/residual decomposition. Regularization is the least intrusive mode and tests whether semantic structure can be internalized by the neural predictor. Projection is the clearest validity mechanism and tests whether hard correction is useful at inference time. Residual dynamics is the strongest structural factorization, because it changes the prediction target by assigning covered dynamics to symbolic rules and residual dynamics to the neural model. This diagnostic separation is important for understanding why a method improves or fails.

\paragraph{When NS-MAWM is scientifically useful.}
NS-MAWM is most appropriate when observations expose repeated semantic regularities, but symbolic knowledge remains incomplete. In that regime, the method creates a controlled interface between what can be specified and what must be learned. If symbolic coverage is too small, the approach should behave close to the neural baseline; if coverage is high but wrong, hard enforcement can hurt. The rule-dropout and correction-magnitude analyses are therefore essential, because they test whether the framework degrades gracefully rather than merely succeeding in domains with convenient rules.

\paragraph{Automating symbolic coverage.}
The preliminary LLM-rule study illustrates a secondary implication of the decomposition: rule acquisition can be treated as a source of partial coverage rather than as an all-or-nothing requirement for a complete symbolic simulator. LLMs are one possible source of such candidates, alongside environment ontologies, rule libraries, program analysis, or human-authored documentation. Future systems could combine retrieval-augmented prompting, structured extraction, rule verification, and coverage auditing before rules enter the NS-MAWM interface. The important point for this paper is narrower: because NS-MAWM explicitly separates covered and residual dynamics, imperfect rule sources can be evaluated through coverage, RVR, correction magnitude, and planning utility rather than assumed correct.

\paragraph{Ablation logic.}
The ablations separate NS-MAWM from simpler explanations. MAWM plus post-correction tests whether inference-time repair alone matches projection and residual variants. The symbolic-feature baseline tests whether exposing rule predicates as inputs is sufficient without enforcing transition consistency. The RSSM/Dreamer-style baseline checks whether a stronger recurrent latent WM removes the need for explicit semantic constraints. Oracle covered-feature validity separates what the rules solve from what residual prediction and planning still require. Finally, rule-dropout, noisy-rule, and OOD evaluations test whether the method degrades in a controlled way as coverage becomes incomplete or configurations shift. Together these comparisons support the interpretation that the gains come from the covered/residual neuro-symbolic interface rather than from symbolic access, repair, or planner changes alone.

\section{Broader Impacts}
\label{sec:broader_impacts}

This work is foundational research on model-based multi-agent learning in simulated environments. Its positive impact is to make imagined multi-agent rollouts more auditable by separating learned predictions from rule-covered semantic constraints. This can help diagnose invalid plans before deployment in domains such as logistics, robotics simulation, or cooperative decision support.

The main risk is misplaced trust. A model can satisfy all covered symbolic rules while still being wrong on residual or hidden variables, and downstream planners may exploit such errors. We therefore recommend reporting rule coverage, pre/post-correction violations, correction magnitude, and downstream invalid-plan rates together, rather than treating symbolic consistency as a blanket safety guarantee.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

NS-MAWM is designed for settings where observations expose enough structure for symbolic rules to apply. It does not assume a complete symbolic simulator, but it does assume that a non-empty subset of features can be covered by correct rules. If the coverage mask is wrong, if rules are misspecified, or if important state variables are hidden from the observation representation, the covered-feature guarantee may no longer hold.

The formal guarantee is intentionally limited: projection and residual symbolic dynamics enforce rule satisfaction only on $\mathcal{F}^d$ and only under correct symbolic transition rules. They do not guarantee lower global prediction error, better residual-feature prediction, or improved downstream return in every environment. A low or zero RVR$_{post}$ can therefore be a superficial validity signal if it is achieved through large corrections, poor residual predictions, or rollouts that are not useful for planning. Reporting RVR$_{pre}$, correction magnitude, residual-feature error, and downstream return together is meant to prevent this overinterpretation. These broader claims require empirical validation, and their strength depends on the planning protocol, rule coverage, environment stochasticity, and the quality of the neural residual model.

The approach also introduces modeling and engineering costs. Rules must currently be specified manually or through a domain-specific symbolic representation, rule coverage must be audited per environment, and symbolic projection can add inference overhead. Scalability with many agents, many feature types, or large rule sets remains an important open question because rule matching, mask construction, and projection may grow with the number of agent-feature-rule interactions. Finally, hard enforcement can create overconfidence: a model may satisfy all covered rules while still producing poor predictions on uncovered features. When coverage is extremely low, or when rules encode constraints that are not informative for downstream decisions, NS-MAWM should behave close to MAWM and should not be expected to deliver large gains.

The residual neural component also remains partly opaque: NS-MAWM makes covered symbolic dynamics auditable, but it does not explain every residual prediction made in latent or structured neural space. The current empirical scope is therefore strongest for structured simulated environments where symbolic regularities are natural to specify. Richer open-ended environments with changing rules, hidden variables, or high stochasticity may require learned rule refinement, ontology-driven rule extraction, or uncertainty-aware symbolic coverage estimates.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

World modeling should separate what can be specified from what must be learned. We proposed \textbf{NS-MAWM}, a neuro-symbolic framework for semantically reliable multi-agent WMs based on this decompositional principle: expose which transition components are rule-covered, leave residual uncertainty to neural dynamics, and couple the two at the transition boundary through regularization, projection, or residual dynamics.

NS-MAWM provides a conditional deductive guarantee on covered features: under correct rules and masks, projection and residual variants eliminate post-correction rule violations on $\mathcal{F}^d$. Claims about long-horizon predictive fidelity, planning/control, robustness, OOD generalization, and data-limited learning are evaluated empirically. The main lesson is that semantic validity should be treated as a first-class property of multi-agent WMs, alongside prediction error, because invalid imagined transitions can change action selection under the same planning budget. RVR, correction magnitude, and downstream planning metrics together provide a reusable diagnostic lens for checking whether validity is learned, enforced locally, or obtained through large repairs. Future work will explore automatic rule extraction or refinement, including domain ontologies and LLM-assisted rule proposals~\cite{Brown2020gpt3,openai2023gpt4}, more scalable symbolic transition representations, and high-performance implementations using vectorized numerical frameworks such as \texttt{JAX}~\cite{jax2018github}.
